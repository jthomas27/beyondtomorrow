## Introduction

In 2026, the world’s geopolitical landscape is being redrawn before our eyes. Artificial Intelligence is transforming the very foundations of power, alliances are shifting in response to new threats and opportunities, and fresh regional conflicts—like the ongoing disruptions in the Strait of Hormuz—are opening up fault lines that threaten global stability. The central question this post explores: **How are technology and conflict reshaping the world order, and what new patterns of power are emerging?**

Gone are the days when military hardware or economic might alone decided a nation’s fate. Today, the ability to wield smart power—including AI, cyber capabilities, and strategic alliances—is becoming the defining feature of global influence. Let’s dive into the tectonic shifts underway, examining how technology, rivalry, and regional instability are forging the geopolitics of tomorrow.

## AI as the New Power Lever

### The Rise of Artificial Intelligence

Artificial Intelligence is not just a technological trend—it’s a geopolitical game-changer. Both the United States and China have made AI a central pillar of their national strategies, recognizing its potential to tip the balance of power ([European Parliament, 2021](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/STUD/2021/662926/IPOL_STU(2021)662926_EN.pdf)).

- **Computing Power as Influence:** The ability to process vast amounts of information and deploy autonomous systems is now treated as a critical lever of national influence. The US and China are racing to outpace each other in AI research, chip manufacturing, and quantum computing ([The Economist, 2025](https://www.economist.com/the-world-ahead/2025/11/10/the-contours-of-21st-century-geopolitics-will-become-clearer-in-2026)).
- **Europe’s Dilemma:** The European Union is scrambling to maintain its relevance in this AI-driven world, strategizing to avoid being sidelined by the US-China rivalry. European lawmakers have pushed for ethical AI standards and investments in homegrown technology, but the continent risks lagging as a mere regulatory power rather than a true innovator ([European Parliament, 2021](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/STUD/2021/662926/IPOL_STU(2021)662926_EN.pdf)).

### Smart Power and New Warfare

AI is also reshaping warfare:

- **Cyber Operations:** Nations deploy AI-driven cyber attacks, targeting infrastructure, finance, and communications. These silent battles often go unseen by the public but carry profound consequences ([Springer, 2023](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-031-...)).
- **Autonomous Weapons:** The development and deployment of autonomous drones and robots are changing military doctrines, reducing the human cost but increasing the unpredictability of conflict.

**Key Insight:** In the new world order, the nation that commands AI not only protects its interests but shapes the rules of engagement for everyone else. The global balance of power is now measured in algorithms and silicon, as much as in tanks and treaties.

## US-China Rivalry: The Axis of Tension

### Intensifying Competition

The US-China rivalry has moved beyond trade wars and diplomatic spats. It’s now the central axis around which global geopolitics revolve, especially in technology ([The Economist, 2025](https://www.economist.com/the-world-ahead/2025/11/10/the-contours-of-21st-century-geopolitics-will-become-clearer-in-2026)).

- **Competing Strategies:** Both nations have unveiled strategies that treat AI, quantum computing, and chip manufacturing as critical to national security. Export controls, sanctions, and tech alliances are the new tools of statecraft.
- **Global Impact:** Other nations must navigate this rivalry, choosing sides or attempting to carve out independent paths. The EU, India, and Southeast Asian nations are each making moves to avoid being caught in the crossfire, with mixed success.

### Technology as a Fault Line

- **Supply Chains:** The battle for dominance in semiconductors and AI has fragmented global supply chains. Countries dependent on imports face new vulnerabilities if they fall out of favor with either camp.
- **Standards and Ethics:** The US and China are setting competing standards for AI ethics and data governance, potentially leading to a bifurcated internet and divergent technological ecosystems.
- **Export Controls as Geopolitics:** The United States has escalated chip controls, restricting access to advanced semiconductors for Chinese firms. China has responded by doubling down on domestic semiconductor capacity and deepening ties with nations willing to accept Huawei infrastructure. These technology sanctions have become a form of economic warfare with cascading effects on global manufacturing and innovation networks. Countries caught in between — particularly in Southeast Asia and the Middle East — face difficult choices about which technological ecosystem to align with.

**Question to Consider:** Will the world split into two competing technological spheres, or can a multipolar order emerge in the shadow of US-China competition?

## BRICS and the Rise of the Global South

### A Parallel Order Taking Shape

While the US-China rivalry dominates headlines, a quieter but significant restructuring is underway across the developing world. BRICS — the grouping that originally comprised Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa — expanded dramatically in 2024 and 2025, bringing in Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Iran, Egypt, and several others. By 2026, the bloc represents roughly 45% of the world's population and over a third of global GDP ([Carnegie Endowment, 2025](https://carnegieendowment.org/research/2025/03/brics-expansion-and-the-future-of-world-order-perspectives-from-member-states-partners-and-aspirants?lang=en)).

The expansion reflects a genuine appetite among Global South nations for an alternative to Western-dominated economic institutions. BRICS members have accelerated discussions on trading in local currencies, reducing dependence on the US dollar and sidestepping Western sanctions regimes. De-dollarisation is still far from practical reality — dollar pricing continues to dominate commodities markets — but the political intent is unmistakable.

### Diverging Visions Within the Bloc

Not everyone inside BRICS shares the same agenda. India, for instance, maintains a careful balancing act: it is simultaneously a founding BRICS member, a Quad security partner with the US, and an independent strategic actor. Brazil under Lula has pursued selective engagement rather than ideological alignment. The result is a bloc that projects pluralism but frequently struggles to act in concert.

What BRICS does offer, however, is a legitimacy resource — a platform for countries that want more say in global governance without fully committing to either the US or Chinese orbit. For smaller nations, this hedging strategy has become increasingly attractive.

## Regional Conflicts: The Strait of Hormuz and Beyond

### New Fault Lines

While technology grabs headlines, regional conflicts are generating new geopolitical fault lines. The US-Israeli war in the Strait of Hormuz is a prime example ([Reuters, 2026](https://www.reuters.com/world/iran-war-live-six-tankers-attacked-gulf-strait-hormuz-2026-03-12/)):

- **Disrupted Trade:** Attacks on tankers have threatened one of the world’s most critical energy corridors, sending shockwaves through global markets.
- **Wider Instability:** These local conflicts risk triggering broader confrontations, as alliances are tested and new blocs form. The Gulf states, Iran, and external powers like Russia are all maneuvering in response.

### Energy Security and Alliances

- **Energy as Leverage:** Control of energy routes remains a potent geopolitical tool. In 2026, disruptions to oil and gas flows can destabilize economies far beyond the Middle East.
- **Shifting Alliances:** The war has driven new alignments, with some nations seeking closer ties to the US or China, while others hedge their bets or pursue neutrality.

**Evidence:** Six tankers attacked in March 2026 highlighted the fragility of global trade and the importance of regional stability ([Reuters, 2026](https://www.reuters.com/world/iran-war-live-six-tankers-attacked-gulf-strait-hormuz-2026-03-12/)).

## Alliances: Adaptation and Fragmentation

### Old Alliances, New Realities

Traditional alliances like NATO and the EU are under pressure:

- **Internal Divisions:** Members disagree over how to respond to technological threats and regional instability. The EU faces tension between regulatory ambitions and the need for innovation.
- **Expansion and Retrenchment:** Some alliances seek to expand their influence, while others retrench, focusing on domestic resilience.

### New Blocs and Multipolarity

- **Emerging Powers:** India, Brazil, and other rising nations are asserting themselves, forming new blocs and partnerships.
- **Flexible Arrangements:** Alliances are increasingly fluid, driven by issue-specific cooperation rather than broad ideological bonds.

**Unverified Claim:** There are rumors of emerging security pacts in Southeast Asia, but concrete evidence remains elusive.

## The Central Issue: Technology and Conflict Redrawing the Map

Across all these trends, one central issue stands out: **The interplay between technology and conflict is fundamentally reshaping global power, alliances, and fault lines.**

- **AI and cyber capabilities are the new currency of influence.**
- **Regional conflicts create new vulnerabilities and realignments.**
- **Traditional alliances are adapting, fragmenting, or reinventing themselves.**

The world order in 2026 is less about clear blocs and more about dynamic networks—where power flows through technology, information, and strategic partnerships as much as through armies and economies.

## What Comes Next?

The shifting geopolitical order is far from settled. The coming years will likely see:

- **Greater competition for AI leadership and technological standards** — the race is accelerating, and laggards face structural disadvantages that compound over time.
- **Continued instability in energy corridors and regional hotspots** — the Hormuz disruptions are a warning sign, not an aberration.
- **A more fragmented, multipolar world with shifting alliances and new blocs** — the binary Cold War framework no longer maps onto current realities; we are entering an era of strategic ambiguity.
- **BRICS as a force for institutional reform** — or a vehicle for fragmentation, depending on whether member states can translate shared discontent into coherent governance alternatives.
- **Climate geopolitics rising** — as resource scarcity and extreme weather events increase, they will generate new friction over migration, food security, and energy access that no amount of diplomatic goodwill can easily resolve.

For policymakers, business leaders, and ordinary citizens, the challenge is to adapt to a world where the rules are changing — and the players are multiplying. The unipolar moment is over. What replaces it will be contested, messy, and full of opportunity for those who can read the new map of power clearly. The choices made now — in boardrooms, parliaments, and research labs — will define not just national fortunes, but the very nature of global cooperation and conflict for the decades ahead.
